How to Hire Filipino Workers for Ireland in 2026: The Employer's Guide

25 May 2026  ·  9 min read  ·  By CA Recruitment

Most Irish care homes, farms, and hotel kitchens that have hired Filipino workers say the same thing afterward: the process was more straightforward than they expected. The timeline is 6 to 8 months. The steps are defined. The same route is used by hundreds of Irish employers every year.

This guide explains every stage — the Irish permit application, what the Philippines side involves, the D-visa, and what happens when the worker arrives. By the end, you will know exactly what is involved and whether it makes sense for your business.

Why Filipino workers?

Irish employers across care, agriculture, construction, and hospitality hire Filipino workers for the same reasons: English proficiency, strong vocational training backgrounds, and a structured overseas employment system that makes the departure process predictable.

The Philippines has one of the most developed overseas employment programmes in the world. Workers are medically screened, documented, and oriented before they leave. When a Filipino worker arrives in Ireland, they typically settle quickly — they have been prepared for the move. This matters when you are trying to get someone productive in a care home, on a farm, or on a construction site with minimal disruption.

For care roles specifically, many Filipino applicants hold nursing degrees from Philippine universities. That level of clinical training applied to an HCA role means faster onboarding and lower supervision requirements than you might expect.

Step 1 — Find a DMW-accredited agency

The Philippines' Department of Migrant Workers (DMW) — formerly known as the POEA — regulates the overseas employment of Filipino nationals. For a Filipino worker to legally depart the Philippines for employment abroad, the placement must be handled through a DMW-accredited agency.

This matters to Irish employers for a practical reason: if your agency does not have DMW accreditation for Ireland, the worker cannot legally depart under the Philippines' overseas employment programme. They would need to travel on a visitor visa and convert status after arrival — a much more complicated and legally uncertain route.

How to verify: ask the agency for their DMW licence number and the markets they are accredited for. A legitimate agency will provide this without hesitation. You can also verify directly with the DMW.

CA Recruitment is DMW-accredited for Ireland placements. This is not optional infrastructure — it is how the process works legally on the Philippine side.

Why this matters more than it appears: Several Irish recruitment agencies advertise Filipino workers but outsource the Philippines-side process to a local partner. If that partner lacks proper DMW accreditation, the placement is legally exposed. Ask the question directly.

Step 2 — Complete the Labour Market Needs Test

Before you can apply for a General Employment Permit (GEP), you must demonstrate that no suitable EEA candidate is available for the role. This is the Labour Market Needs Test (LMNT).

The LMNT requires advertising the vacancy for 28 consecutive days on two platforms:

  • Jobs Ireland / DSP Employment Services — free (this satisfies the mandatory DSP/EURES network requirement)
  • At least one additional commercial job platform

You keep a log of every application received and document why EEA applicants were not selected. DETE checks this record when you submit the permit application. The 28-day period is fixed — there is no shortcut.

You can run candidate sourcing in parallel with the LMNT. While the Irish advertising is running, we can be screening Filipino candidates so that when the test period closes, you already have a preferred candidate ready for the permit application stage.

Step 3 — Apply for the General Employment Permit

After the LMNT is complete, the GEP application goes to DETE through the Employment Permits Online portal. This is the employer's application — the employer provides the signed job offer, LMNT records, tax registration details, and current workforce information (for the 50/50 ratio check).

Key requirements for the application:

  • The role must be on DETE's Eligible Occupations List
  • Minimum annual salary of €36,605 for most roles (€32,691 for healthcare support, horticulture, and meat processing)
  • At least 50% of your total workforce must be EEA nationals at the time of application
  • LMNT records showing 28 days of advertising and the outcome

DETE's current processing time for new GEP applications is approximately 10 to 12 weeks — check the live processing dates at enterprise.gov.ie before planning your timeline, as this changes regularly. The DETE application fee is €1,000, paid at submission. This fee is the employer's cost — you cannot recover it from the worker.

We prepare and submit the full application on behalf of our clients. The employer signs documents and reviews the draft application, but does not manage the process directly.

Step 4 — Worker documents from the Philippines

While the DETE application is being processed, the worker is preparing their Philippine-side documentation. These are worker-side costs and responsibilities, but your agency should be tracking them:

  • NBI clearance — the Philippines' National Bureau of Investigation clearance, equivalent to an Irish Garda clearance. DETE requires this for the permit application.
  • Medical certificate — obtained from a DMW-accredited clinic in the Philippines. Confirms the worker is medically fit for employment abroad.
  • OEC (Overseas Employment Certificate) — issued by the DMW once the placement is confirmed. The worker cannot legally depart without it. It exempts them from departure taxes.
  • Educational credentials — degree certificates, transcripts, or professional licences relevant to the role, to be presented to DETE and the Irish employer.

For care roles, the worker's nursing degree or healthcare qualification is also assessed at this stage. If your care home requires NMBI registration (for nurses rather than care assistants), that is a separate process that NMBI manages directly with the worker.

Step 5 — Irish D-visa application

Once DETE approves the GEP, the worker applies for their Irish D-visa (long-stay employment visa) at the Irish Embassy in Manila. This is the worker's application, not the employer's — you provide a copy of the approved employment permit, but the worker submits the visa application.

Required documents for the D-visa include the approved employment permit, passport, medical certificate, NBI clearance, and evidence of the employment offer. The Irish Embassy in Manila processes the application — current turnaround is typically 2 to 4 weeks, though this can vary.

The D-visa is a single-entry visa that allows the worker to enter Ireland and begin employment. On arrival, they must register with Immigration Service Delivery (ISD) to receive their Irish Residence Permit (IRP) card, which formalises their legal status in Ireland.

Step 6 — Arrival and onboarding

When the worker arrives in Ireland, there are several practical steps to complete quickly:

  • PPS number — the worker needs a Personal Public Service number to be put on payroll, access public services, and register with the Revenue Commissioners. This is obtained from a local Intreo/Social Welfare office. Bring the employment permit and passport.
  • IRP registration — the worker registers with Immigration Service Delivery (ISD) within 90 days of arrival to receive the Irish Residence Permit card. This formalises their right to work in Ireland under the permit.
  • Bank account — needed to receive wages. Some banks are more straightforward for new arrivals than others. An Garda Síochána letter confirming the worker's registered address can help in some cases.
  • Garda vetting (for care roles) — the National Vetting Bureau processes this after arrival. No fee to the employer. Allow a few weeks.

We support workers through this process. Most of it can be done within the first week of arrival if the PPS number appointment is booked in advance.

Care sector specifics

If you are hiring Filipino care workers for a nursing home or home care service, there are two additional considerations:

QQI Level 5 equivalency. Care assistant roles in Ireland typically require a QQI Level 5 in Healthcare Support or equivalent. Filipino nursing graduates (BSN holders) generally meet this threshold. DETE assesses qualifications at application stage. If the worker has a nursing degree, document this clearly in the application.

NMBI for registered nurses. If you are hiring a nurse for a role that requires NMBI registration (rather than an HCA), the process is different. The worker applies to the Nursing and Midwifery Board of Ireland directly for registration. NMBI assesses qualifications, may require adaptation periods or exams depending on the applicant's background, and grants registration independently of the employment permit. This takes additional time — factor it into your recruitment timeline if you are hiring nurses specifically.

Frequently asked questions

What is DMW accreditation and why does it matter for Irish employers?

The DMW (Department of Migrant Workers) is the Philippine body that regulates overseas employment of Filipino nationals. For a Filipino worker to legally depart the Philippines for work abroad, the placement must go through a DMW-accredited agency for that destination country. Without this, the worker cannot depart through the official overseas employment programme. Verify your agency's DMW accreditation before proceeding.

What is the OEC and does the employer need to do anything about it?

The OEC (Overseas Employment Certificate) is a DMW document confirming the worker is a documented overseas Filipino worker. The worker obtains it before departure. The employer does not apply for it, but your agency should confirm it is in place. Without it, the worker can face issues at Manila airport.

What Irish visa does a Filipino worker need?

A long-stay D-visa, applied for at the Irish Embassy in Manila after the employment permit is approved. The employer provides the approved permit; the worker submits the visa application. Turnaround is typically 2 to 4 weeks. On arrival in Ireland, the worker registers with Immigration Service Delivery (ISD) to receive their Irish Residence Permit (IRP).

How long does it take to hire Filipino workers for Ireland?

6 to 8 months end-to-end. That covers 28 days for the LMNT, approximately 10 to 12 weeks of DETE processing (check current dates at enterprise.gov.ie), 2 to 4 weeks for the D-visa, and travel plus onboarding time. Parallel stages keep it towards the shorter end.

Do I need a solicitor to hire Filipino workers in Ireland?

For most straightforward GEP applications, no. A competent agency handles the permit without legal input. A solicitor may be needed for complex business structures or if there have been previous WRC compliance issues.
