How to Hire Overseas Care Assistants for Your Irish Care Home or Service

25 May 2026  ·  8 min read  ·  By CA Recruitment

Irish care homes and home care providers are running out of local options. The staffing shortage is not a new conversation, but what has changed in 2026 is that overseas recruitment is no longer the last resort — it is an active part of how well-run care services are keeping their rosters covered. DETE issued 924 healthcare employment permits in April 2026 alone. This guide explains exactly how the process works for care employers.

Why Irish care providers are hiring from overseas

The numbers are not ambiguous. A May 2026 IMT survey found that 43% of Irish nursing homes reported they were rarely or never adequately staffed. Pay rates have improved, but the pool of available care workers locally is not growing fast enough to fill the gap. Home care providers face the same picture.

Overseas recruitment, done properly, fills roles that would otherwise sit empty for months. Filipino care workers in particular have strong backgrounds for this work: many hold nursing degrees, most are English-speaking, and the Philippines has a structured departure process through the Department of Migrant Workers (DMW) that makes the transition more predictable than you might expect.

What qualifications do overseas care assistants need?

For a care assistant role in an Irish nursing home or home care service, the standard benchmark is a qualification equivalent to QQI Level 5 in Healthcare Support.

Filipino workers with a Bachelor of Science in Nursing (BSN) degree consistently meet this requirement. The BSN is a four-year undergraduate degree, and DETE assesses qualifications at permit application stage — BSN holders have been accepted for care assistant GEP applications in practice. This is one reason Filipino candidates are well-suited to Irish care roles — they are often overqualified for a care assistant position, which means they tend to progress quickly once established.

English language is also assessed, though DETE does not mandate a specific standardised test score for care assistant GEP applications. The practical requirement is that the worker can communicate effectively in a care setting. Most Filipino applicants are educated in English from primary level and have no difficulty meeting this standard.

For nursing roles specifically: If you want to hire a registered nurse rather than a care assistant, the permit route is different and NMBI registration is required. This is a separate process from the GEP care assistant route. Contact us to discuss nursing hire separately.

Which employment permit applies?

Care assistant roles are eligible for a General Employment Permit (GEP). The role is on DETE's Eligible Occupations List under healthcare support.

The minimum annual salary for care assistant GEP applications is €32,691. This is the lower threshold that DETE applies to healthcare support roles and is lower than the standard GEP minimum of €36,605. You need to be paying at or above €32,691 for the permit to issue.

The GEP is valid for up to 24 months and can be renewed. After sufficient time working in Ireland on employment permits, workers can progress to long-term residency status — the specifics depend on the worker's circumstances and are handled through Immigration Service Delivery.

The Labour Market Needs Test

Before applying for a GEP, you must complete the Labour Market Needs Test (LMNT). This requires you to advertise the care assistant vacancy for 28 consecutive days on two platforms:

  • Jobs Ireland / DSP Employment Services — free (this satisfies the mandatory DSP/EURES network requirement)
  • At least one additional commercial job platform

You keep records of every application received during the 28 days and document why any EEA candidates were not selected. DETE checks this evidence at application stage.

For most care home operators, the LMNT is straightforward — you run the advertising, receive few or no suitable local applications, and document accordingly. The 28-day period is unavoidable but can run while you are preparing the rest of the application.

The 50/50 workforce rule

At the time you submit the GEP application, at least 50% of your total workforce must be EEA nationals. This covers all staff across the business — care workers, administration, management, kitchen, cleaning. Everyone counts.

For most care homes that have been operating for some time, this is not a problem. If you are close to the 50% threshold from previous overseas hires, you need to check the ratio before starting the LMNT. A refused application wastes 28 days of advertising and the DETE fee.

We check the 50/50 ratio as the first step in every free consultation. There is no point going further in the process if the ratio won't be met. See our guide to the 50/50 rule for a full explanation of how it is calculated and what exceptions exist.

Timeline: what to expect

Allow 6 to 8 months from starting the process to the worker's first day in your care home. Here is how that breaks down:

  • 28 days — LMNT advertising period
  • 10 to 12 weeks — DETE processing of the GEP application (check current processing dates at enterprise.gov.ie — this changes regularly)
  • 2 to 4 weeks — Worker applies for Irish D-visa at the Irish Embassy in Manila (after permit is issued)
  • 1 to 2 weeks — Travel, arrival, initial onboarding

Garda vetting runs in parallel with the post-permit visa stage. The National Vetting Bureau handles this — there is no fee to the employer. Allow a few weeks for it to come through.

Why Filipino care workers specifically?

We place Filipino care workers across Ireland because the fit with the Irish care sector is strong. The main reasons:

  • Nursing backgrounds. A significant proportion of Filipino care worker applicants hold nursing degrees. They are trained for clinical environments and understand care protocols from day one.
  • English proficiency. The Philippines uses English as a medium of instruction from primary school. Communication with residents, families, and colleagues is not a barrier.
  • Stability. The Philippines has a structured overseas employment system through the DMW (Department of Migrant Workers). Workers go through medical checks, documentation, and orientation before departure. They have made a significant commitment before they arrive.
  • Track record in Irish care settings. Filipino workers are already working in care homes across Munster, Leinster, and Connacht. This is not an untested proposition for Irish employers.

Garda vetting and NBI clearance

Every overseas care worker needs two things before starting in an Irish care setting:

NBI clearance — the Philippines' National Bureau of Investigation clearance, equivalent to a criminal background check. This is required by DETE as part of the permit application. The worker obtains it in the Philippines before departure.

Garda vetting — run through Ireland's National Vetting Bureau. There is no fee to the employer. The worker's NBI clearance is separate from Garda vetting and does not substitute for it — both are required. Garda vetting typically takes a few weeks and can be initiated after the worker's visa and travel are confirmed.

Frequently asked questions

What qualifications do overseas care assistants need to work in Ireland?

A qualification equivalent to QQI Level 5 in Healthcare Support. For Filipino workers, a BSN (Bachelor of Science in Nursing) is widely accepted. Sufficient English for the care setting is also required.

What work permit do overseas care assistants need in Ireland?

A General Employment Permit (GEP). Care assistant roles are on DETE's Eligible Occupations List. The minimum salary threshold for care assistant GEPs is €32,691 per year from 1 March 2026.

How long does it take to hire an overseas care worker in Ireland?

Allow 6 to 8 months from starting the process to the worker's first day. That covers the 28-day LMNT, approximately 10 to 12 weeks of DETE processing (check current processing dates at enterprise.gov.ie), visa application, and travel.

How much does it cost to hire an overseas care assistant in Ireland?

The DETE fee is €1,000. LMNT advertising typically runs €200 to €500. With CA Recruitment, the recruitment fee is agreed upfront and is separate from these government costs. The worker's salary must be at least €32,691 per year.

Do overseas care workers need Garda vetting in Ireland?

Yes. Garda vetting is required for anyone working in a care setting. The National Vetting Bureau handles it — no charge to the employer. The worker's NBI clearance from the Philippines is a separate requirement handled before departure.
