The 50/50 Rule for Irish Employment Permits — What It Means for Your Business

23 April 2026  ·  6 min read  ·  By CA Recruitment

The 50/50 rule is one of the first things employers hit when they start researching employment permits — and one of the most misunderstood. It's cited as a barrier, a loophole, and a reason to give up, often by people who don't fully understand how it works. This article explains it plainly.

What the 50/50 rule actually is

Under Irish employment permit legislation, an employer must ensure that at least 50% of their workforce — across the entire business — consists of EEA nationals. This applies at the point of applying for a General Employment Permit (GEP).

So if you have 10 employees and want to bring in a non-EEA worker on a GEP, you need at least 5 of your existing 10 employees to be EEA nationals. The proposed new hire counts as the 11th, and the 6 EEA employees out of 11 keeps you above 50%.

The calculation covers your entire business, not just one location or department. If you run four restaurants across Co. Clare and Co. Galway, every employee across all four sites counts. You can't apply the rule to a single location in isolation.

Who it blocks — and who it doesn't

For most established Irish businesses with a settled workforce, the 50/50 rule isn't a problem. If you run a hotel with 40 staff and most of them are Irish, EU, or EEA nationals, you're well above the threshold and the rule doesn't restrict you.

It tends to be an issue for businesses that have already brought in multiple non-EEA workers through previous permits and are now approaching or have crossed the 50% threshold. At that point, DETE won't issue another GEP until the balance shifts — either through new EEA hires or natural turnover.

It's also a concern for very small businesses — say, a family farm with 2 full-time employees — where a single non-EEA hire would push the proportion above 50%.

The two exceptions that actually exist

There are only two situations where DETE will waive the 50/50 requirement for a GEP. Employers sometimes get told there are others — there aren't.

1. Sole employee

If the non-EEA worker will be the only employee in the business, the 50/50 rule doesn't apply. This covers a self-employed person hiring their first employee who happens to be non-EEA.

2. Start-up businesses with formal EI or IDA support

A business that was established less than two years ago and holds a formal letter of support from Enterprise Ireland or IDA Ireland can apply for a GEP without meeting the 50/50 requirement. The letter must specifically support the business — not just acknowledge it exists.

Business size alone is not an exemption. There is no "fewer than X employees" exemption in Irish employment permit law. If you've been told otherwise, that information is wrong. DETE checks the ratio at application stage, and an application that doesn't meet the requirement will be refused.

The Critical Skills route — what actually changes

The 50/50 rule applies to both the General Employment Permit and the Critical Skills Employment Permit (CSEP). If you switch to the CSEP route, the ratio requirement doesn't go away.

What the CSEP does skip is the Labour Market Needs Test — so if your business qualifies for CSEP (role on the Critical Skills Occupations List at €40,904+, or salary of €68,911 or above regardless of role), you cut 28 days of mandatory advertising out of the process. That's a meaningful saving, but you still need the 50/50 ratio in place. Nurses, medical scientists, certain engineers, and some IT roles qualify by occupation. Senior roles in many sectors can qualify by salary threshold alone.

What this means in practice

Before we submit a GEP application for any employer, we check the 50/50 ratio as part of the free consultation. It's one of the first things we look at because there's no point going through the Labour Market Needs Test (which takes 28 days minimum) if the application is going to be refused at the ratio check.

If your ratio is borderline, there are a few ways to approach it — we'll walk through the options with you. If the CSEP route is available for the role, we'll tell you that upfront.

The short version: the 50/50 rule doesn't stop most employers from hiring non-EEA workers. But you need to know where you stand before you start the process.
