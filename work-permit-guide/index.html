How to Hire Overseas Workers in Ireland: Step-by-Step Guide for Employers

A plain-English explanation of Irish employment permits — which type you need, what the salary thresholds are, how the Labour Market Needs Test works, and what happens at every stage of the DETE application process.

Updated: April 2026 Reading time: ~10 minutes Source: enterprise.gov.ie (DETE)

What is an Irish Employment Permit?

An Irish employment permit is a legal authorisation that allows a non-EEA national (including citizens of countries outside the EU, EEA, and Switzerland) to work in Ireland in a specific job with a specific employer.

Employment permits are issued by the Department of Enterprise, Trade and Employment (DETE). Without a valid permit, it is illegal to employ a non-EEA national in Ireland — and both the employer and the worker can face serious consequences if this rule is breached.

Filipino nationals require an employment permit to work in Ireland. As Irish employers face a significant and ongoing labour shortage in many sectors, the employment permit system is the main legal route for hiring overseas workers.

Important: Employment permits are tied to a specific employer and a specific role. If a worker changes job or employer, they generally need a new permit application.

The Two Main Permit Types

There are nine categories of employment permit in Ireland, but for most employers hiring skilled Filipino workers, the two relevant options are:

1. Critical Skills Employment Permit

The Critical Skills Employment Permit (CSEP) is designed for high-demand, skilled occupations. It is the more employer-friendly of the two main permit types because:

  • No labour market needs test is required — you do not need to advertise the role locally first
  • It is typically processed faster than a General Employment Permit
  • Workers on a CSEP can apply for a Dependant/Partner/Spouse Employment Permit (DPSEP), allowing their family to join them and work in Ireland without restriction
  • When a CSEP expires, the holder can apply directly to the Department of Justice for Stamp 4 rather than renewing their permit — this is a significant advantage of the CSEP route

To qualify for a CSEP, the role must generally pay at least €40,904 per year and appear on the Critical Skills Occupations List maintained by DETE. Some roles above €68,911 per year may qualify regardless of the occupation list.

2. General Employment Permit

The General Employment Permit (GEP) is the broader route available for a wider range of occupations. Key points:

  • A labour market needs test is required — the role must be advertised for a minimum of 28 consecutive days on Jobs Ireland (jobsireland.ie) and one additional online platform before applying
  • The minimum salary threshold is generally €36,605 per year, though some roles in agriculture and food production qualify at a lower rate under specific schemes (e.g. horticulture workers and healthcare assistants at €32,691)
  • Certain occupations are on the Ineligible Occupations List and cannot be filled using a GEP
  • Workers must meet the 50/50 workforce rule (see below)

The 50/50 Rule Explained

One of the most misunderstood requirements in Irish employment permit law is the 50/50 rule, which applies to General Employment Permits.

The rule states that at least 50% of the employees of the employer (across the entire business, not just the specific location or department) must be EEA nationals at the time of the permit application.

In practice, this means: if your business has 10 employees and you want to hire a non-EEA worker on a General Employment Permit, at least 5 of your existing 10 staff must be EEA nationals.

Exceptions to the 50/50 rule

  • Start-ups: Businesses that are less than two years old and hold a formal letter of support from Enterprise Ireland or IDA Ireland may be exempt
  • Sole employee: If the non-EEA worker will be the only employee of the business, the 50/50 rule does not apply
  • Critical Skills route: The 50/50 rule does not apply to Critical Skills Employment Permits

Note: There is no general exemption based on company size or headcount. A business with fewer than 30 employees is not automatically exempt. The only recognised exemptions are those listed above.

Salary Thresholds

Employment permits in Ireland come with minimum salary requirements. These are reviewed periodically by DETE and can change. The thresholds below reflect the position as of April 2026 — always confirm the current rates at the time of your application.

Permit Type Minimum Annual Salary Notes
Critical Skills Employment Permit €40,904 Role must appear on the CSEP Occupations List; degree or higher required
Critical Skills (any occupation) €68,911 No occupation list restriction above this threshold
General Employment Permit (most roles) €36,605 Labour market needs test required
General Employment Permit (horticulture / HCA / meat processing) €32,691 Specific lower threshold — minimum hourly rate €16.12 applies

Note: Salary thresholds are updated periodically by DETE. The figures above are based on publicly available information at the time of writing. Always verify current thresholds before submitting an application — or let us do it for you.

The Labour Market Needs Test

Before applying for a General Employment Permit, you are required to demonstrate that the role could not be filled from within the EEA labour market. This is called the Labour Market Needs Test (LMNT), and it involves:

  1. Advertising the role on the Department of Social Protection's Jobs Ireland service (jobsireland.ie), which also publishes to the EURES European jobs network, for a minimum of 28 consecutive days
  2. Advertising the role on one additional online platform (a jobs website, newspaper website, or recruitment site) for the same 28-day period
  3. Recording the outcome — including any EEA applicants who applied and why they were not suitable

Realistic Timelines

One of the most common questions we get is: how long will this take? The honest answer is that it depends — on the permit type, the current DETE processing queue, the visa processing time, and the candidate's individual circumstances.

As a general guide:

  • Critical Skills Employment Permit: Typically 2–3 months from permit application submission to permit approval (processing times vary and can be longer during peak periods)
  • General Employment Permit: Typically 3–4 months from permit application, plus the 2-week LMNT advertising period before application
  • Total end-to-end timeline (from first conversation to first day): Most placements complete within 3–6 months

These timelines are estimates based on typical cases. DETE processing times fluctuate, and individual visa circumstances can add time. We will always give you an honest, realistic timeline at the start of our engagement — not an optimistic one designed to get you to sign up.

Your Obligations as an Employer

Taking on a non-EEA worker under an employment permit comes with specific legal obligations. These include:

  • Paying at least the minimum annual salary stated on the permit
  • Providing the same employment rights and conditions as any other employee
  • Not charging the employee the cost of the employment permit (this is illegal under Irish law)
  • Notifying DETE within 28 days if the employment ends before the permit expires
  • Not requiring the employee to work in a different role or location than specified on the permit without obtaining an amended permit
  • Keeping records of the employee's right to work documentation

CA Recruitment briefs you on all of these obligations as part of the onboarding process. We are also available to answer compliance questions at any point during the worker's employment.

Family Members & Dependants

Workers on a Critical Skills Employment Permit can apply for their spouse, civil partner, or dependent children to join them in Ireland under the Dependant/Partner/Spouse Employment Permit (DPSEP). This permit allows family members to work in Ireland in any role without restriction — a significant advantage of the CSEP route.

Workers on a General Employment Permit may also be eligible for family reunification under separate immigration rules, typically after 12 months of lawful residence in Ireland.
