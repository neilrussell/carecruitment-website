<p>Most guides to Irish work permits tell you which roles you <em>can</em> fill from overseas. This one answers the more painful question: which roles you <strong>can't</strong> — at any salary, on any permit.</p>

<p>Ireland keeps an official Ineligible List of Occupations for Employment Permits. If the job you're trying to fill is on it, a non-EEA worker cannot be sponsored for that role, full stop. Employers find this out the hard way — usually after weeks of advertising and a refused application. This is the list, the exceptions that catch people out, what changed in May 2026, and what your options are if your role is on it.</p>

## The short answer {#short-answer}

The Ineligible List of Occupations is the Irish State's list of jobs that are closed to employment permits. It's set out in Schedule 4 of the Employment Permits Regulations and maintained by <a href="https://enterprise.gov.ie/en/what-we-do/workplace-and-skills/employment-permits/employment-permit-eligibility/ineligible-categories-of-employment/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">DETE — Ireland's Department of Enterprise, Trade and Employment</a>.

<p>One fact drives everything else on this page: <strong>if an occupation is on the ineligible list, no salary makes it eligible.</strong> A General Employment Permit won't be granted. A Critical Skills permit won't be granted. Even the high-salary route that lets you hire for unlisted occupations at €68,911 or above explicitly excludes anything on the ineligible list. The door is closed unless a specific exception applies.</p>

<p>The list is built on Standard Occupational Classification (SOC) codes, not job titles. That matters more than it sounds — the role you call a "site supervisor" or a "kitchen manager" maps to an occupation code, and it's the code that decides eligibility, not your wording on the advert.</p>

## The three tiers explained {#three-tiers}

Every occupation in Ireland sits in one of three tiers for permit purposes. Knowing which tier your role is in tells you almost everything about how hard it will be to hire.

<ul>
<li><strong>Critical Skills (the green light).</strong> Roles on the Critical Skills Occupations List — registered nurses, engineers, software developers and the like. No Labour Market Needs Test, immediate family reunification, a path to Stamp 4. See <a href="/blog/critical-skills-list-ireland-employer-check-2026/">our employer check on the Critical Skills list</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Eligible, but not critical (the amber light).</strong> The large middle ground — care assistants, chefs in proper kitchens, dairy operatives, HGV drivers, general operatives. These go through the General Employment Permit, with a 28-day Labour Market Needs Test first. Workable, just slower.</li>
<li><strong>Ineligible (the red light).</strong> Roles on the ineligible list. No permit route is open to them, regardless of pay.</li>
</ul>

<div class="callout callout-gold">
<p><strong>The mistake to avoid:</strong> assuming a role is "skilled enough" to qualify. Eligibility has nothing to do with how important the job is to your business. A receptionist your office can't run without is still ineligible; a steel fixer on a building site is now eligible. The list is the list.</p>
</div>

## What's on the ineligible list {#on-the-list}

The list is long and works at the level of occupation groups. Rather than reproduce every SOC code, here are the categories Irish employers most often run into when they discover a role is blocked:

<ul>
<li><strong>Sales, retail and customer service:</strong> retail assistants, cashiers, sales supervisors, call-centre operators, estate agents.</li>
<li><strong>Office and administration:</strong> receptionists, secretaries, office managers, bookkeepers, payroll managers, credit controllers, bank clerks.</li>
<li><strong>Hospitality and leisure (front of house):</strong> bar staff, waiting staff, hotel and hospitality managers, leisure and sports facility managers, travel agency managers.</li>
<li><strong>Personal services:</strong> hairdressers, beauticians, childcare workers and nursery nurses.</li>
<li><strong>Elementary roles:</strong> cleaners, window cleaners, security guards, kitchen and catering assistants, hospital porters, refuse workers.</li>
<li><strong>Many trades and operatives:</strong> a range of construction operatives, process and machine operatives, and general agricultural workers.</li>
</ul>

<p>Two whole categories are closed without exception: all <strong>work in a private home</strong> and <strong>domestic operative</strong> roles. A live-in carer or housekeeper hired into a family home cannot be sponsored on a standard permit.</p>

<p>If your role appears in one of these groups, don't give up yet — read the next section first. The exceptions are where a lot of "ineligible" roles quietly become hireable.</p>

## The exceptions employers miss {#exceptions}

This is the part that pays for the read. Several broad categories are ineligible *except* for specific roles carved out within them. Employers routinely write off a job that an exception actually covers.

<ul>
<li><strong>Chefs.</strong> Listed as ineligible — but experienced executive, head, sous and commis chefs are eligible, as long as the establishment is not a fast-food or takeaway outlet. A sous chef in a hotel restaurant is hireable; a fast-food cook is not.</li>
<li><strong>Agriculture.</strong> General agricultural workers are ineligible, but <strong>dairy farm assistants and pig farm assistants are specifically eligible</strong> — which is exactly why so many Irish farms can hire from overseas.</li>
<li><strong>HGV and bus drivers.</strong> Ineligible as a general category, with an exception for drivers holding the appropriate Irish licence — opening the door for the haulage sector.</li>
<li><strong>Construction.</strong> Most operative roles are ineligible, but the 2026 amendment carved out steel fixers, plastic lining technicians, fencing operators and erectors, curtain wallers and concrete pump operators.</li>
<li><strong>Fishing.</strong> Sea fishers in the Irish fishing fleet are eligible, along with a capped number of fish filleters (a 50-permit quota) and seafood operatives (a 100-permit quota).</li>
<li><strong>Care.</strong> Health care assistants and home support workers are no longer ineligible — they're hired through the General Employment Permit, which is why nursing homes and home-care providers can now recruit overseas.</li>
</ul>

<p>The lesson is consistent: the occupation code and the exact role wording decide it. Two jobs with near-identical titles can land on opposite sides of the line. If there's any doubt, confirm the role's status before you advertise a salary or pay an application fee.</p>

## What changed in May 2026 {#may-2026}

The list isn't fixed. It's reviewed by DETE, and the most recent change came through the <strong>Employment Permits (Amendment) Regulations 2026</strong>, which took effect on <strong>22 May 2026</strong>.

<p>Five occupations were removed from the ineligible list entirely, meaning they can now be filled on a permit:</p>

<ul>
<li>Pharmaceutical technicians</li>
<li>Dental technicians</li>
<li>Printers</li>
<li>Textile process operatives</li>
<li>Forestry workers</li>
</ul>

<p>The same amendment added the construction and fishing exceptions listed above, plus lineworkers for ESB Networks and industrial machine knitters. It's a reminder that "ineligible" isn't permanent — a role blocked last year can be open this year. If you were told no in the past, it's worth checking again.</p>

## What to do if your role is ineligible {#what-to-do}

If your exact role really is on the list, you still have moves. In order:

<ol>
<li><strong>Check for an exception first.</strong> The single most common error is writing a role off as ineligible when a carve-out covers it. Confirm the occupation code and the specific role wording before you conclude anything.</li>
<li><strong>Look at how the role is defined.</strong> A job genuinely operating at a higher skill or seniority level may map to an eligible occupation. This has to reflect the real duties — not a paper relabelling — but roles are often pitched lower than the work actually is.</li>
<li><strong>Concentrate local and EEA hiring on the blocked roles.</strong> EEA nationals don't need a permit, so the roles you can't fill from outside the EEA are the ones to push hardest through Irish and EU channels.</li>
</ol>
