<p>Pig units are among the hardest farms in Ireland to staff. The work is skilled, the hours are structured around the animals, and the local labour pool for farrowing and husbandry roles has been shrinking for years. If you run a pig farm and you've been asking whether you can hire from outside the EU — the answer is yes, and the route is more direct than for most other farm types.</p>

<p>Pig farm assistants and pig managers are both eligible for a General Employment Permit (GEP) from DETE. No quota applies, so there's no cap waiting to close on you. This guide covers the salary rules specific to pig roles, the process from first call to a worker on your unit, and what it costs.</p>

<h2 id="can-pig-farms-hire">Can Irish pig farms hire workers from overseas?</h2>

<p>Yes. Most farm worker roles sit on DETE's ineligible occupations list, which is why many farmers assume overseas hiring is off the table. Pig farming is one of the named exceptions.</p>

<p>DETE's ineligible list carves out two pig-specific roles:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Pig farm assistant</strong> — an exception under farm workers (SOC 9111)</li>
  <li><strong>Pig manager</strong> — an exception under farmers (SOC 5111)</li>
</ul>

<p>Both were removed from the ineligible list in December 2023, when the government expanded the permit system specifically to bring skills into what it called these niche roles. The route is established, it is being used, and DETE processes these applications on an ongoing basis.</p>

<p>What this means in practice: feeding, farrowing, weaning, health monitoring, and unit maintenance roles can be filled with an overseas worker on a GEP. A general farm labourer role cannot — the job on the permit application needs to be genuine pig unit work.</p>

<h2 id="roles">Which pig farm roles qualify?</h2>

<p>The two eligible classifications cover most of what a commercial pig unit needs:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Pig farm assistant</strong> — day-to-day operative work: feeding, cleaning, farrowing house duties, weaning, moving stock, health checks, and unit upkeep</li>
  <li><strong>Pig manager</strong> — running a unit or section: breeding programme management, herd performance, staff supervision, and compliance</li>
</ul>

<p>If the role you're hiring for doesn't fit neatly into either, <a href="https://wa.me/353894166124?text=Hi%2C%20I%20run%20a%20pig%20farm%20and%20I%27m%20looking%20to%20hire%20workers%20from%20overseas.%20Can%20you%20help%3F" target="_blank" rel="noopener">WhatsApp Monette</a> before you do anything else. Classifying the role correctly at the start is the difference between a clean application and a refusal.</p>

<h2 id="salary">The salary rules — and a common mistake</h2>

<p>Here is the detail that catches people out. Some agricultural roles qualify for a reduced GEP salary threshold of €32,691 per year. <strong>Pig roles are not among them.</strong> DETE applies the reduced rate only to horticulture workers, meat processor operatives, healthcare assistants, and home support workers.</p>

<p>For pig farm assistants and pig managers, the standard GEP minimum applies:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Minimum annual remuneration: €36,605</strong> — the standard GEP rate, in force since 1 March 2026</li>
  <li>That works out at <strong>€18.05 per hour on a 39-hour week</strong></li>
  <li>The figure must be met through <strong>basic pay</strong>. Overtime, bonuses, and shift allowances do not count towards it</li>
</ul>

<p>Permits are not restricted to full-time-only contracts — DETE issues them for 20 to 48 hours a week. The annual figure is the binding floor: on fewer than 39 hours, the hourly rate has to rise so the annual minimum is still met; above 39 hours, you pay at least the €18.05 hourly rate, so the annual figure climbs with the hours.</p>

<p>If you want to sense-check a specific contract before committing, our <a href="/tools/salary-checker/">salary checker</a> does the arithmetic for you. DETE checks the contracted pay at application stage — get it wrong and the application is refused.</p>

<h2 id="no-quota">No quota on pig roles — and why that matters</h2>

<p>Several agricultural permit categories are quota-capped. Dairy farm assistant permits, for example, are limited to a fixed number — when the quota fills, DETE rejects further applications until a new one is announced. Farmers who started the process late have lost months to a closed quota.</p>

<p>Pig farm assistant and pig manager permits carry no quota. You can apply in any month, there is no annual cap, and no risk of the door closing while your application is being prepared. For a sector where staffing gaps tend to hit suddenly — a key worker leaving mid-cycle — that flexibility matters.</p>

<h2 id="process">The process, step by step</h2>

<p>Four stages. CA Recruitment manages all of them — you sign documents and interview candidates; you don't touch the portal or the paperwork.</p>

<h3>Step 1 — Eligibility and the 50/50 rule</h3>

<p>At application date, at least 50% of your total workforce must be EEA nationals. For a pig unit employing four or five people and hiring one or two overseas workers, this is rarely a problem — but it has to be checked before anything else, because DETE will not process an application that fails it.</p>

<p>The only exemptions: businesses where the overseas worker will be the sole employee, and companies registered with Revenue in the last two years holding a formal letter of support from Enterprise Ireland or IDA Ireland. There is no general small-business exemption. We run this check on the first call, at no cost.</p>

<h3>Step 2 — Labour Market Needs Test (28 days)</h3>

<p>Before the permit application, you must show that no suitable EEA candidate is available. The vacancy is advertised for 28 consecutive days on Jobs Ireland (the DSP/EURES platform) plus at least one additional online platform. Every application received is logged, with reasons why no EEA candidate was suitable, and the permit application must go in within 90 days of the first advertisement.</p>

<p>We run the LMNT for you — and source Filipino candidates in parallel, so when the 28 days close you already have a shortlist of people with pig unit experience.</p>

<h3>Step 3 — GEP application to DETE</h3>

<p>The application goes through DETE's Employment Permits Online system. The fee is <strong>€1,000</strong> for a permit of up to 24 months, paid by the employer — it cannot be charged to or recovered from the worker. If DETE refuses the application, 90% of the fee is refunded.</p>

<p>Current processing time for new GEP applications is roughly 10 to 12 weeks. Check the <a href="https://enterprise.gov.ie/en/what-we-do/workplace-and-skills/employment-permits/current-application-processing-dates/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">live processing dates at enterprise.gov.ie</a> — this figure moves.</p>

<h3>Step 4 — D-visa, arrival, and onboarding</h3>

<p>Once the permit is approved, the worker applies for an Irish D-visa at the Irish Embassy in Manila — typically 2 to 4 weeks. Filipino workers also complete the Philippine side: medical screening and an Overseas Employment Certificate from the Department of Migrant Workers (DMW). We track all of it.</p>

<p>On arrival, the worker registers with Immigration Service Delivery (ISD) for their Irish Residence Permit (IRP) and gets a PPS number for payroll. Most of it is done within the first week or two if appointments are booked ahead.</p>
