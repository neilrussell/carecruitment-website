<p>Irish contractors are winning work they cannot fully staff. Sites are running short of welders, pipe fitters, steel fixers, and qualified trades, and the domestic pipeline is not refilling fast enough to change that. The roles are open. The skilled people to fill them locally are not there in the numbers the sector needs.</p>

<p>This is a structural shortage, not a seasonal dip. And for employers who have already exhausted local and EEA hiring, there is a clear, legal route to fill skilled roles: the overseas employment permit. This post sets out the scale of the gap, which trades are hardest to fill, and exactly how the permit route works, including the change that opened more construction trades in June 2026.</p>

<h2 id="the-shortage">The Scale of the Construction Labour Shortage</h2>

<p>The numbers are stark. <strong>Property Industry Ireland estimates the construction sector needs between 95,000 and 110,000 additional workers by 2030</strong> to deliver the Government's target of 300,000 new homes under Housing for All. That works out at roughly 25,000 new workers a year for the rest of the decade.</p>

<p>The current workforce can't close that gap on its own:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>The sector still sits below its peak.</strong> Construction employment never fully recovered after the 2008 crash, when a generation of trades left the industry or the country.</li>
<li><strong>The workforce is ageing.</strong> Around a fifth of current construction workers are expected to retire within the next decade, so the gap widens before it closes.</li>
<li><strong>Demand keeps rising.</strong> Housing, retrofitting, and infrastructure targets all pull on the same limited pool of skilled labour at once.</li>
</ul>

<p>For many contractors the result is simple and costly: you tender for work, you win it, and then you cannot build the team to deliver it. That is the problem the overseas route exists to solve.</p>

<h2 id="hardest">Which Trades Are Hardest to Fill</h2>

<p>The shortage is not even across the sector. The roles employers struggle with most are the skilled, qualification-backed trades, which are also the roles that qualify for a permit. The ones we are asked about most often are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Welders and pipe fitters</strong> — especially for mechanical and process work, where MEP contractors are turning down jobs.</li>
<li><strong>Steel fixers</strong> — newly opened to the overseas route in 2026 (more on that below).</li>
<li><strong>Electricians and plumbers</strong> — covered by their own sectoral pay orders and in constant demand.</li>
<li><strong>Carpenters and shuttering carpenters</strong> — a thin local pool, particularly outside the cities.</li>
<li><strong>Engineers</strong> — mechanical, electrical, and civil engineering roles that sit on the skilled lists.</li>
</ul>

<p>If the role needs a recognised trade qualification or a professional engineering background, it is very likely eligible. Basic, unskilled site labour is the exception, as it stays on DETE's ineligible list and cannot be sponsored.</p>

<h2 id="why-local">Why Local Hiring Isn't Filling Sites</h2>

<p>Plenty of contractors have done everything right locally. They have advertised the role, offered competitive site rates, and still ended up short. There are a few structural reasons local hiring keeps falling short.</p>

<p><strong>The pool is smaller than the demand.</strong> When every contractor in the region is chasing the same welders and fixers, most lose out. Advertising harder does not create a tradesperson who is not there.</p>

<p><strong>Wage inflation alone doesn't fix it.</strong> Bidding up rates moves scarce workers between sites rather than adding new skilled people to the sector.</p>

<p><strong>EEA recruitment has limits.</strong> Hiring from within the EEA is the obvious first step, and most employers try it. But the same shortage runs across much of Europe, so it often does not produce the candidate either.</p>

<p>Once you have genuinely exhausted the local and EEA market, the overseas permit route is the option that actually fills the role. That is exactly what it is for.</p>

<h2 id="the-route">The Overseas Route: The General Employment Permit</h2>

<p>Construction trades sit <strong>outside the Critical Skills list</strong>. That has one important consequence for employers: skilled construction roles are filled through the <a href="/work-permit-guide/">General Employment Permit (GEP)</a>, the standard permit issued by DETE (Ireland's Department of Enterprise, Tourism and Employment), and the GEP route requires a Labour Market Needs Test first.</p>

<p>That single point, the LMNT requirement, is where most contractors get stuck or get it wrong. It is also exactly what CA Recruitment manages for you. We cover the LMNT in plain English below.</p>

<p>The worker placed on a GEP has the same employment rights as any Irish or EEA worker. That is a legal requirement under the Employment Permits Acts, enforced by the WRC (Workplace Relations Commission).</p>

<h2 id="june-2026">More Trades Opened to the Permit Route in June 2026</h2>

<p>The timing matters. On 28 May 2026, DETE announced a set of changes to the occupations lists, and several construction roles were opened to the General Employment Permit. <strong>Steel fixers and concrete pump operators were removed from the ineligible list</strong>, alongside plastic lining technicians, fencing operators, and curtain wallers. These five trades carry no quota, so they are eligible straight away. Construction planner/scheduler and geospatial surveyor roles were added to the Critical Skills list.</p>

<p>Separately, certain quota-based occupations in the same update cannot be applied for until <strong>10 June 2026</strong>, to allow the Labour Market Needs Test to be completed first. That date does not hold up the quota-free trades above.</p>

<p>In plain terms: if you have been unable to hire a steel fixer or a concrete pump operator locally, that role is now eligible for the overseas route, provided you run the LMNT correctly. You can read the full process in our guide on <a href="/blog/how-to-hire-construction-workers-from-overseas-ireland/">how to hire construction workers from overseas in Ireland</a>.</p>

<h2 id="lmnt">The LMNT in Plain English</h2>

<p>The Labour Market Needs Test sounds technical. It is not. It is the step where you prove to DETE that no suitable EEA worker was available before you hire from outside the EEA.</p>

<p>Here is what it actually involves:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Advertise the role for 28 consecutive days.</strong> The ad runs on Jobs Ireland (the State's jobsireland.ie platform) and at least one other online job board.</li>
<li><strong>Keep a record of every applicant.</strong> You log who applied and note why no EEA candidate was suitable for the role.</li>
<li><strong>Submit that record with the permit.</strong> DETE checks the LMNT evidence when the application is reviewed.</li>
</ul>

<p>The 28-day period is fixed. There is no shortcut. But the work itself, advertising, logging, and documenting, is exactly what CA Recruitment handles for you, so it does not land on your site manager's desk. And the smart move is to run candidate sourcing in parallel: while the LMNT clock runs, we are already screening tradespeople, so you have a preferred candidate ready the day it closes.</p>

<h2 id="pay">What You Have to Pay a Construction Worker on a Permit</h2>

<p>This is the question every contractor asks, and the answer for construction is not the standard permit floor. Construction workers are covered by the <strong>Construction Sector Sectoral Employment Order (SEO)</strong>, a legally binding set of minimum rates that sits <em>above</em> the general €36,605 GEP threshold.</p>

<p>The SEO rates are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>€23.00 per hour for craftspersons</strong> from 1 August 2025, about €46,600 a year on a 39-hour week.</li>
<li><strong>€22.32 per hour for Category A workers.</strong></li>
<li><strong>€20.71 per hour for Category B workers.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>The SEO rate has to be paid as contracted basic pay. You cannot reach it by stacking overtime or site allowances on top of a lower base, and DETE will check that at application stage. Electricians and plumbers fall under their own separate sectoral orders, so check the rate for the specific trade. The current figures are published at <a href="https://www.workplacerelations.ie/en/what_you_should_know/hours-and-wages/sectoral%20employment%20orders/construction-sector/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">workplacerelations.ie</a>. For the full picture across roles, see our guide to the <a href="/blog/minimum-salary-overseas-worker-ireland/">minimum salary for an overseas worker in Ireland</a>.</p>

<h2 id="timeline">A Realistic Timeline</h2>

<p>Plan for around <strong>6 to 8 months from first contact to a worker on site</strong>. Know that going in and you won't be caught out. That full window covers:</p>

<ul>
<li>The 28-day Labour Market Needs Test.</li>
<li>The DETE permit application and its processing queue. Check the <a href="https://enterprise.gov.ie/en/what-we-do/workplace-and-skills/employment-permits/current-application-processing-dates/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">live processing dates at enterprise.gov.ie</a>, as this figure changes.</li>
<li>The worker's long-stay visa and travel to Ireland.</li>
</ul>

<p>It is not a process measured in weeks, and any agency that tells you otherwise is not being straight with you. But it is a dependable way to fill a skilled role that local advertising has not. Running stages in parallel, sourcing during the LMNT and Philippine documentation during DETE processing, keeps it toward the shorter end of that range.</p>
