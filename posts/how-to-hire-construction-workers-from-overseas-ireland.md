<p>Irish construction companies are signing contracts they cannot fully staff. Engineers are short. Pipefitters and welders are short. <p>Hiring skilled construction workers from overseas is legal, structured, and more straightforward than most contractors assume. <h2 id="can-i-hire">Can Irish construction employers hire workers from overseas?</h2>

<p>Yes — for skilled and professional roles. The Irish employment permit system distinguishes between skilled occupations and elementary labour, and that distinction matters for construction employers.</p>

<p>Roles that qualify for a <a href="/work-permit-guide/">General Employment Permit (GEP)</a> from DETE (Ireland's Department of Enterprise, Trade and Employment) include:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Mechanical engineer</li>
  <li>Electrical engineer</li>
  <li>Civil engineer</li>
  <li>Welder</li>
  <li>Pipe fitter</li>
  <li>Steel fixer</li>
  <li>MEP and structural engineering roles</li>
</ul>

<p>What does not qualify: basic construction operatives (SOC 9120 — elementary construction occupations) and road or rail construction operatives are on DETE's ineligible list. If the role requires no formal qualification and consists of general site labour, it cannot be sponsored under a GEP.</p>

<h2 id="the-process">The hiring process: what it involves</h2>

<p>There are four main stages. The total timeline is 4 to 6 months from first contact to a worker starting on site.</p>

<h3 id="step-1-eligibility">Step 1 — Eligibility and role classification</h3>

<p>Before anything else, confirm that the role is GEP-eligible and that your business meets the 50/50 rule. DETE will not process a permit application for a company where fewer than 50% of the total workforce are EEA nationals at the time of application.</p>

<p>The only exemptions to the 50/50 rule are businesses registered with Revenue in the last two years that hold a formal letter of support from Enterprise Ireland or IDA Ireland, and situations where the non-EEA worker will be the sole employee. Most established contractors do not qualify for an exemption — check your headcount before starting the process.</p>

<p>This is a quick check. CA Recruitment runs it at the start of every engagement, at no cost.</p>

<h3 id="step-2-lmnt">Step 2 — Labour Market Needs Test (28 days)</h3>

<p>Before applying for a GEP, you must demonstrate that no suitable EEA candidate is available. This is the Labour Market Needs Test (LMNT) — a mandatory 28-day advertising period.</p>

<p>The vacancy must be posted on:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Jobs Ireland</strong> — the DSP/EURES employment network (jobsireland.ie). This is the mandatory platform.</li>
  <li><strong>At least one additional online platform</strong> — a commercial job board, LinkedIn, or any other site whose principal purpose is publishing job offers.</li>
</ul>

<p>Both ads must run for the full 28 consecutive days. You keep a log of every application received and document why no EEA applicant was selected. DETE checks this record when you submit the permit application. The 28-day period is fixed — there is no shortcut.</p>

<p>Run your candidate sourcing in parallel. While the LMNT is running, CA Recruitment is screening Filipino candidates. When the 28-day period closes, you already have a preferred candidate ready.</p>

<h3 id="step-3-gep">Step 3 — GEP application to DETE</h3>

<p>After the LMNT closes, the GEP application goes to DETE through the Employment Permits Online system. The employer applies — the worker does not apply for their own permit.</p>

<p>Key requirements at this stage:</p>
<ul>
  <li>The role must meet the minimum annual salary of <strong>€36,605</strong>. This is the standard GEP threshold — there is no lower construction-specific rate.</li>
  <li>The 50/50 rule must still be met at application date.</li>
  <li>LMNT records must be submitted — the full 28-day advertising log and outcome.</li>
  <li>The employer's signed job offer, tax clearance, and business registration are required.</li>
</ul>

<p>The application fee is <strong>€1,000</strong> for a permit of up to 24 months. If DETE refuses the application, 90% of the fee is refunded. The employer pays this cost — it cannot be charged to or recovered from the worker.</p>

<p>Current DETE processing time for new GEP applications is approximately 10 to 12 weeks. Check the <a href="https://enterprise.gov.ie/en/what-we-do/workplace-and-skills/employment-permits/current-application-processing-dates/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">live processing dates at enterprise.gov.ie</a> before planning your schedule — this figure changes regularly.</p>

<p>CA Recruitment prepares and submits the complete application on behalf of our clients. You sign documents and review the draft; you do not manage the portal or the paperwork directly.</p>

<h3 id="step-4-dvisa">Step 4 — D-visa and arrival</h3>

<p>Once DETE approves the GEP, the worker applies for an Irish D-visa (long-stay employment visa) at the Irish Embassy in Manila. This is the worker's application — the employer provides a copy of the approved permit, but does not submit the visa application.</p>

<p>Required documents for the D-visa include the approved employment permit, passport, a medical certificate from a DMW-accredited clinic, and evidence of the signed employment offer. The Irish Embassy in Manila typically processes D-visa applications within 2 to 4 weeks.</p>

<p>Filipino workers are also required to obtain an Overseas Employment Certificate (OEC) from the Philippine Department of Migrant Workers (DMW) before departing. This is a worker-side document — CA Recruitment tracks it to make sure it is in place before travel.</p>

<p>On arrival in Ireland, the worker must register with Immigration Service Delivery (ISD) to receive their Irish Residence Permit (IRP) card, which formalises their legal right to work. They will also need a PPS number to go on payroll, obtained from a local Intreo office.</p>

<h2 id="why-filipino">Why Filipino workers for Irish construction</h2>

<p>The Philippines has one of the most developed overseas employment systems in the world. Construction and engineering trades are well-established routes for Filipino workers, and the skills base is genuine — not a credential gap dressed up for migration purposes.</p>

<p>Filipino construction workers bring:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>English proficiency.</strong> English is a medium of instruction throughout Philippine secondary and tertiary education. Site communication, toolbox talks, and safety briefings work from day one.</li>
  <li><strong>Vocational and technical training.</strong> TESDA (Technical Education and Skills Development Authority) regulates trade qualifications in the Philippines. Welders, pipe fitters, and electricians hold nationally recognised certifications that can be assessed against Irish and EU standards.</li>
  <li><strong>Structured departure.</strong> The DMW (Department of Migrant Workers) regulates overseas employment. Workers are medically screened and oriented before leaving the Philippines. When they arrive on your site, the process behind their placement has been checked on both ends.</li>
  <li><strong>Retention.</strong> Overseas Filipino workers typically stay. They have committed to a contract, relocated their life, and have family support structures that depend on the income. Casual turnover is not the pattern you see.</li>
</ul>
