<p>Ireland's kitchens are short of chefs, and 2026 has not changed that. Restaurants, hotels, and gastropubs across the country are advertising for head chefs, sous chefs, and chefs de partie for months at a time and getting few — sometimes no — suitable applicants. The roles are open. The trained people to fill them locally are not there in the numbers the industry needs.</p>

<p>The good news for employers is that there is a clear, legal route to fill these roles. Chef positions are eligible for the General Employment Permit, the standard Irish work permit for hospitality. That means a restaurant or hotel can recruit a skilled chef from overseas and bring them into the kitchen lawfully — with the permit and visa process managed in full.</p>

<p>Below is why the shortage won't shift, why local advertising keeps coming up empty, and how the overseas route actually fills a chef role.</p>

<h2 id="the-shortage">The Scale of the Chef Shortage</h2>

<p>The chef shortage is one of the longest-running staffing problems in Irish hospitality. The Restaurants Association of Ireland has long pointed to chef and kitchen-staff shortages as one of the sector's biggest constraints — operators that can't staff a kitchen can't open extra covers, extend hours, or take on a new site.</p>

<p>The pressure is felt across the whole sector:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Independent restaurants</strong> that lose a head chef and then can't replace them, putting the whole business at risk.</li>
<li><strong>Hotels</strong> running multiple food outlets — restaurant, bar, banqueting — that need a full brigade and can't build one.</li>
<li><strong>Gastropubs and country venues</strong> outside the main cities, where the local pool of trained chefs is thinnest.</li>
</ul>

<p>For many operators, the problem is not wages or conditions — it is that the qualified, available chef simply does not exist in the local market when the vacancy opens. Advertising harder doesn't conjure a candidate who isn't there.</p>

<h2 id="why-local">Why Local Hiring Isn't Filling Kitchens</h2>

<p>Plenty of employers have done everything right on the local side. They have advertised the role properly, offered a fair wage, and still ended up with an empty pass. There are a few structural reasons local hiring keeps falling short.</p>

<p><strong>The pool is smaller than the demand.</strong> The number of trained chefs in Ireland has not kept pace with the number of kitchens that need them. When more venues chase the same small group of experienced chefs, most lose out.</p>

<p><strong>Experienced chefs are already employed.</strong> The strongest candidates are rarely on the market. Poaching from another local kitchen often just moves the problem next door rather than solving it.</p>

<p><strong>EU recruitment has its limits.</strong> Hiring from within the EEA is the obvious first step, and many employers try it. But the same shortage exists across much of Europe, so it frequently doesn't produce the candidate either.</p>

<p>Once an employer has genuinely exhausted the local and EEA market, the overseas permit route is the option that actually fills the role. That is exactly what it exists for.</p>

<h2 id="eligible">Chefs Are Eligible for the General Employment Permit</h2>

<p>This is the part many employers don't realise: there is no blanket ban on hiring a non-EU chef in Ireland. Chef roles are eligible for the <strong>General Employment Permit (GEP)</strong> — the standard permit issued by DETE (Ireland's Department of Enterprise, Trade and Employment) for hospitality and most other sectors.</p>

<p>That means a restaurant, hotel, or pub can lawfully recruit a chef from outside the EEA, sponsor them on a GEP, and bring them into the kitchen. The worker has the same employment rights as any Irish or EEA employee — a legal requirement under the Employment Permits Acts, enforced by the WRC (Workplace Relations Commission).</p>

<p>Chef grades that are routinely placed on the GEP route include:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Executive Chef</strong> — 5 years' experience</li>
<li><strong>Head Chef</strong> — 5 years' experience</li>
<li><strong>Sous Chef</strong> — 5 years' experience</li>
<li><strong>Chef de Partie (CDP)</strong> — 2 years' experience</li>
<li><strong>Commis Chef</strong> — 2 years' experience</li>
</ul>

<p>Two conditions matter here. The grade has to carry the minimum experience DETE requires — five years for executive, head and sous chef, two years for chef de partie and commis — and the kitchen can't be a fast-food outlet. CA Recruitment confirms both for your specific role before anything is submitted, so a candidate is never put forward who won't clear the permit stage.</p>

<h2 id="salary">The Salary Threshold and the Route</h2>

<p>For a chef on the General Employment Permit, the salary offered must meet the standard GEP minimum of <strong>€36,605 per year</strong>, based on a 39-hour week. Chefs sit on this standard floor — they are not on a reduced threshold. The role has to be paid at or above this level for the permit to be granted.</p>

<p>For most experienced chef roles — particularly head chef and sous chef positions — this threshold is realistic and often already in line with what the role pays. Check the figure against the hours you're offering — the annual minimum is the number that binds, not the hourly. You can read the detail in our guide to the <a href="/blog/minimum-salary-overseas-worker-ireland/">minimum salary for an overseas worker in Ireland</a>.</p>

<h2 id="process">How the Overseas Hiring Process Works for a Chef</h2>

<p>Hiring a chef from overseas follows a defined sequence. Here is what it looks like for a kitchen role, start to finish.</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Role and salary check.</strong> Confirm the chef grade, the experience the permit requires, and that the salary meets the €36,605 GEP threshold for the hours offered.</li>
<li><strong>Labour Market Needs Test (LMNT).</strong> The role is advertised through the required channels to demonstrate no EEA candidate was available. This is a mandatory step before a GEP application can be submitted.</li>
<li><strong>Candidate sourcing and selection.</strong> A suitable chef is sourced, screened, and matched to the role — with experience evidence prepared for the permit.</li>
<li><strong>DETE permit application.</strong> The General Employment Permit application is prepared, documented, and submitted to DETE, then progresses through the processing queue.</li>
<li><strong>Visa and travel.</strong> Once the permit is granted, the chef applies for their long-stay visa and arranges travel to Ireland.</li>
<li><strong>First day in the kitchen.</strong> The chef arrives and starts.</li>
</ol>

<p>Realistically, plan for around <strong>6 to 8 months from first contact to the chef's first day</strong>. That full window covers the LMNT, the DETE application and its queue, the visa, and travel. It is not a process measured in weeks — but it is a reliable way to fill a role that local advertising has not.</p>
