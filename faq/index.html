Employment Permits & Visas
Yes, completely. We manage the employment permit application, supporting documentation, and liaise with DETE (Ireland) or the Home Office (UK) on your behalf. You don't touch a single form.
The two main permit types for hiring non-EU workers are the Critical Skills Employment Permit and the General Employment Permit. Critical Skills is for higher-skilled roles on the CSEP Occupations List (minimum €40,904 per year, or €68,911 for any role not on the list) and does not require a labour market needs test. The General Employment Permit applies to a wider range of roles (minimum €36,605 per year, with a lower threshold of €32,691 for some roles such as healthcare assistants and horticulture workers) and does require a labour market needs test to show the role couldn't be filled locally. We'll advise which route applies to your specific role.
The 50/50 rule requires that at least 50% of your workforce (across your entire business) must be EEA nationals before you can hold a General Employment Permit for a non-EEA employee. The recognised exceptions are: (1) the non-EEA worker will be the sole employee of the business, or (2) the business is less than two years old and holds a formal letter of support from Enterprise Ireland or IDA Ireland. There is no general exemption based on company size — a small headcount alone does not qualify. We'll assess your situation at consultation and tell you exactly where you stand.
Yes. We're experienced with both Irish Critical Skills and General Employment Permit routes, and the UK Skilled Worker visa route. Wherever you're based, we know the rules and handle the process end to end.
Workers on a Critical Skills Employment Permit can apply for their spouse or partner and dependent children to join them in Ireland under the Dependant/Partner/Spouse Employment Permit scheme. Workers on a General Employment Permit may also be eligible for family reunification after 12 months, depending on their circumstances. We can advise on this during your consultation.

Under Irish law, employment permit fees must be paid by the employer. It is illegal for an employer to recover the cost of an employment permit from the worker. The permit application fees are set by the Department of Enterprise, Trade and Employment (DETE) and vary by permit type and duration. We'll give you a full breakdown of expected government fees at consultation.
Employer Obligations
Irish employers of non-EU workers must pay at least the minimum wage (or the permit salary threshold, whichever is higher), provide the same employment rights as any other employee, not charge the worker for the permit, and notify DETE if the employment ends before the permit expires. You must also ensure the worker has the right to work before they start — we manage all of this as part of our service.
For a General Employment Permit, yes — you must demonstrate that the role was advertised for a minimum of 28 consecutive days on Jobs Ireland (the Department of Social Protection's employment service, which also publishes to EURES) and on one additional online jobs platform before applying. For a Critical Skills Employment Permit, no labour market needs test is required. We manage this requirement as part of the permit process.
If a worker leaves before their permit expires, you are required to notify DETE within 28 days. The permit cannot be transferred to another worker — a new permit application would be required. If this happens within the first 90 days of a CA Recruitment placement, we replace the worker at no additional cost.
