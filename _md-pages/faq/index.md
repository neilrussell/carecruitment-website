## The Process

**How does overseas recruitment work?**
The process starts with a free consultation to understand your role and whether overseas recruitment works for your situation. CA Recruitment then manages the employment permit application — including the Labour Market Needs Test advertising if required — and coordinates the visa once the permit is approved. **Is hiring overseas workers right for my business?**
Not always — and CA Recruitment will say so honestly if it is not. Overseas recruitment makes the most sense when you have genuine, ongoing difficulty filling roles locally, when the role meets the salary threshold for a permit (from €36,605 for a General Employment Permit, with lower rates for healthcare and horticulture shortage occupations), when your workforce already has at least 50% EEA nationals, and when you can commit to a 3 to 8 month process before the worker starts.

**Do you work with both Irish and UK employers?**
Yes. CA Recruitment handles Irish employment permit routes (both Critical Skills and General Employment Permits) and the UK Skilled Worker visa route.

**What do I need to do as the employer?**
Agree the terms of employment (salary, role, location, start date); sign the employment permit application as the sponsoring employer; have a valid employment contract ready; and be available for the video interview with shortlisted candidates.

**Can I choose the candidate?**

## Costs and Fees

**How much does it cost to hire an overseas worker?**
CA Recruitment's recruitment fee is discussed at the initial consultation and depends on the role type and volume. No fee is charged until a worker is successfully placed. Separately, the employer pays the government permit fee directly to DETE — currently €1,000 for a General Employment Permit of up to 24 months duration.

**Do I pay anything upfront?**
Nothing. No consultation fee, no retainer, no deposit. CA Recruitment's fee is only charged when a worker is successfully placed.

**What ongoing costs should I expect?**
The main ongoing cost is the worker's salary — minimum €36,605 per year for a standard GEP, or €16.12 per hour (€32,691 per year on a 39-hour week) for healthcare support workers and home carers. Standard employer PRSI applies. The government permit fee is a one-off cost. No agency fees until placement.

**Who pays for the employment permit?**
The employer pays the permit fee. It is illegal under Irish law to recover this cost from the worker — passing permit costs on to a non-EU worker is a criminal offence under the Employment Permits Acts.

## Permits and Visas

**What is the difference between a permit and a visa?**
An employment permit is issued by DETE and authorises a specific non-EEA national to work in a specific role with a specific employer. A visa is issued by the Irish Naturalisation and Immigration Service (INIS) and allows the person to enter and remain in the country. Both are required. The permit is obtained first; the visa application follows once the permit is approved.

**Who applies for the employment permit?**
Either the employer or the worker can apply under Irish law. In practice, CA Recruitment makes the application on the employer's behalf.

**What types of employment permits are available in Ireland?**
The two main types are the Critical Skills Employment Permit (CSEP) and the General Employment Permit (GEP). The CSEP is for higher-skilled roles on the CSEP Occupations List — no LMNT required, faster processing, Stamp 4 pathway. The GEP covers a much wider range of occupations and requires the Labour Market Needs Test.

**What is the Labour Market Needs Test?**
The LMNT is an advertising requirement for General Employment Permit applications. You must advertise the role for a minimum of 28 consecutive days on Jobs Ireland (jobsireland.ie/EURES) and at least one additional online platform, document all applications received, and record reasons for not progressing Irish or EEA candidates.

**Do all roles require a Labour Market Needs Test?**
No. The LMNT is only required for General Employment Permits. Critical Skills Employment Permits are exempt.

**What is the 50/50 rule for employment permits?**
The 50/50 rule requires that at least 50% of your total workforce must be EEA nationals before you can hold a General Employment Permit for a non-EEA worker. It applies across your entire business, regardless of size or location. Two exceptions: (1) the non-EEA worker will be the sole employee, or (2) EI/IDA-backed start-up within 2 years with a formal support letter.

**Can family members of the worker come to Ireland?**
CSEP holders can apply for their spouse, partner, and dependent children to join them under the Dependant/Partner/Spouse Employment Permit scheme. GEP holders do not have the same automatic family reunification pathway during the initial permit period.

## Timelines

**How long does the process take from start to finish?**
Critical Skills Employment Permit: typically 2 to 4 months end-to-end. General Employment Permit: typically 6 to 8 months for care and hospitality; 6 to 8 months for agriculture and construction. The GEP includes the mandatory 28-day LMNT before the application can be submitted, followed by 8 to 12 weeks of DETE processing.

**What causes delays?**
Missing or incorrect documentation at application stage; DETE processing backlogs (live processing dates published at enterprise.gov.ie); delays in the Irish visa application; and issues with the 50/50 ratio discovered after the LMNT has started.

## Compliance and Obligations

**What are my obligations as an employer of a non-EU worker?**
Pay at least the National Minimum Wage (€14.15/hour from January 2026) or the permit salary threshold, whichever is higher. Provide the worker with the same employment rights as any other employee under Irish law. Notify DETE within 4 weeks if employment ends before the permit expires.

**Do I need to advertise the role before applying for a permit?**
For a General Employment Permit, yes — the 28-day Labour Market Needs Test is mandatory. For a Critical Skills Employment Permit, no advertising is required.

**What if the worker leaves before the permit expires?**
Both the employer and the permit holder must notify DETE within 4 weeks if employment ends before the permit expires, and a copy of the permit must be submitted to DETE. The permit cannot be transferred to a new employer.

**What if the worker decides to leave for another job?**
Both GEP and CSEP holders must complete 9 months with their initial sponsoring employer before they can legally change employer in Ireland. After that period, a change of employer requires a formal process — a new permit application in most cases.

**What happens if the permit is refused?**
Refusals usually occur because the role does not meet the salary threshold, the LMNT was not properly conducted, or the employer does not satisfy the 50/50 rule. DETE issues a formal refusal with reasons. CA Recruitment advises on whether to appeal or reapply.
